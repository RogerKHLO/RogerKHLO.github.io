The Generalist
Our correspondent sat down with Roger this week to discuss the philosophy of engineering, the risks of the physical world, and the hidden breakthroughs powering modern tech.
How do you define your engineering approach?

I’m a pragmatic generalist with a real soft spot for the weird and wonderful. I treat mechanical, electrical, and software problems as one cohesive puzzle, though I’m actively working to check my biases and sharpen my fundamentals.

Right now, I’m still exploring where to specialize, but I’m honestly in awe of extreme optimization. I’m looking closely at the intersection of Bio-Mechatronics and Material Science—everything from the raw efficiency of custom ASICs to the organic nature of compliant mechanics and Mycelium Memristors.

I have high hopes that the future won't be about "brute force" engineering. I want to help build systems that are efficient, compliant, and almost feel alive—like electro-adhesive soft robotics.

"Scientists study the world as it is; engineers create the world that has never been." — Theodore von Karman Why is "failure analysis" so critical to your work?

Because in my line of work, a "bug" doesn't just crash an app—it can burn down a building! We have to design for the real world, which is messy. Tolerances drift, manufacturing creates errors, and environments are hostile. Software unit tests are cool, but hardware needs rigorous safety factors and fail-safes. Whether it's code overheating a battery or a loose wire causing a short, the consequences of bad engineering are thermal—and you can't "Control-Z" a fire.

Where does your inspiration come from?

I tend to work like a runaway train—no brakes, just pure linear focus. But honestly? The "Eureka!" moments never happen then. They hit me when I’m doing absolutely nothing—during a quiet conversation, washing dishes, or just walking down the street. It’s those random, "unproductive" moments where the chaos in my brain suddenly aligns into a perfect solution.

"Any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic." — Arthur C. Clarke What is your critique of the current tech landscape?

We’re too obsessed with raw processing power and not enough with clever efficiency. True innovation often hides in the most unglamorous places. Look at Ajinomoto—a food company making MSG! They realized a byproduct of their fermentation process makes the perfect insulator for CPUs, and now the entire chip industry depends on them. That is the kind of cross-disciplinary, waste-conscious thinking we need more of.
